The Premise

What the state took away, the land remembered.

*Sacred Geography* is not just a personal memoir. It is a Gramscian exploration of resilience and subjectivity, tracing the lineage of Cape Town’s English elite alongside the ancestral cosmology of the BaMankge people in Limpopo.

Subjectivity as Resilience

The state could remove people from their ancestral farms, stripping them of cattle, fields, and physical space. But it could not erase the cosmology that lived in the water, the ancestral links, and the rainmaking lineage. The BaMankge subjectivity survived Apartheid because it was rooted in a grammar far older than colonialism.

The Double Inheritance

Tony Harding tracks his own background: growing up in the leafy, Anglican grammar of Rondebosch, Cape Town—his great-great-grandfather’s name etched onto the chapel at Bishops campus—while working in 1998 to return 2,100 hectares of stolen land to the Gamawela community in Limpopo's platinum valleys.

The Sacred Threshold

In September 2006, after a colon cancer operation turned septic, Tony was laid in an ICU ward in Johannesburg. At this threshold of life and death, the physical boundaries of the world gave way to something ancient, spiritual, and structural. This memoir is what he brought to that threshold, and what he found there.

"The state could remove people from the land. It could not remove the land from the people’s understanding of who they were."

— Tony Harding, Sacred Geography

Chapter One: The Inheritance

Register: restrained memoir — specific, sensory, without sentiment.

I grew up as an Anglican. That sentence contains more history than I knew how to carry as a child. It arrived at me through several generations of careful assembly — a name anglicised in Barbados, a family converted in Bath, a grandfather who commanded ships in the Royal Navy and married the daughter of an Archdeacon in Cape Town. By the time it reached me, in the white suburb of Rondebosch, it was simply what we were.

His name is on the chapel at the heart of the Bishops campus. I walked past it every day for years. I did not yet know how to read what I was walking past. My mother, Rosemary Barrow, knew. She relished the connection — relished it with the Headmaster, specifically, who understood what the Brooke name meant in the architecture of the school and of Cape Anglican life. It earned my parents a seat beside him at the athletics pavilion when I competed in the 800 metres. I was always second. Heavy calves from mountain climbing.

My mother did not worship at St Paul's Church in Rondebosch — one of Richard Brooke's parishes, the congregation at the heart of the suburb. She chose St Thomas Church on the Bishops boundary instead. It was a territorial claim. She could not quite reach the centre. She held the edge of what was claimable and held it firmly. She had married down. Wallace Harding was a builder — removed from Bishops as soon as he obtained a lower certificate, apprenticed to his maternal uncle's firm. Harpers and Harding.

That is what the Anglican grammar of Rondebosch looked like from inside. Calibrated to the millimeter, built on old navy ships, seamstresses from Bath, and chapels that bore our lineage.

Chapter Two: The Claim

Theme: Undoing the dispossession of Sekhukhuneland.

The story of Maruping Mankge runs parallel to converso trauma. Name change. Dispossession. Expulsion. Not 1492 — but the same grammar, applied by a different administration to a different people on a different continent. What apartheid did to the Gamawela community was not unique in world history. It was entirely ordinary in its logic. That is what makes it what it is. In 1998 we began the work of undoing it.

The land claims commission required detailed community research before it would accept a claim. This meant going to the people — not to the lawyers, not to the archive, but to the families scattered across the villages of Sekhukhuneland by decades of forced removal and flight. We held small meetings. Mare. Eenzaam. MaSeven. Jane Furse. Tsimanyane. Kokotela. Village after village, family after family, assembling the record of what had been taken and from whom.

Then the farms. We had to visit every property across the catchment that had fallen under Mankge jurisdiction — land that had since passed into the hands of mining companies, game farmers, lodge developers. Pakaneng on the high escarpment, which had been the community's defensive line against Swazi marauding. Diepgezit — the name means hidden place, and it was. Draaikraal. Hermansdal. Helena. Der Brochen. I cannot name all of them. The list is long and each name carries a history the current occupants have not been told.

The two valleys — Klein Dwars and Dwars — ran above a geological fault line. That fault line had exposed platinum, chrome, rhodium, and a range of other metals. Glencore. Anglo American Platinum. African Rainbow Minerals. Implats. Nobody in those negotiations had anticipated that ordinary labour tenants and farmworkers — people the system had stripped of everything except their memory of the land — would take them on in court.

Chapter Three: The Year the Land Came Back

Theme: 2006 — restoration, loss, and the ICU threshold.

Three things happened in 2006. In February, Maruping Isaac Mankge died. He was my father-in-law, heir of the rainmaker's line, the man the community called Tate. He did not live to see what came next. In July, the Land Claims Court issued an order restoring surface rights to the farm St George 2JT — 2,100 hectares in the Dwars River Valley, where Gabolekane had made rain, where the sacred springs still ran, where the divination pots were in their cave on the hillside. The order bore the name of the Gamawela community. It had taken eight years from the lodgement of the claim. Maruping should have held it.

In September, I was in an ICU ward. A colon cancer operation had turned septic. At the threshold, fish came. Tate had grown up the son of Mpurana — deaf and mute, later blind — on the farm the community had been stripped of in stages: the fields first, then the cattle, then the people. Maruping fled. Under his own force, to the white-owned orange farms in Groblersdal. He took a different name when he got there. Rantho — the name of a neighbouring community. Maruping took their name and went to work the citrus. The heir of Gabolekane's rainmaking line, on a white farm in Groblersdal, under a borrowed name.

We spoke Afrikaans to each other, Tate and I. Into the Afrikaans I inserted what I had managed to construct of Sepedi — the Sepedi of the Northern Sotho grammar book. First the names of objects, then the verbs, then the sentence connections between them. It was slow, deliberate work. Just like the return of the land.

The Author

Tony Harding

Tony Harding is a South African writer and advocate of land reform. Raised in the English-speaking, Anglican enclave of Rondebosch, Cape Town, and educated at Bishops, his life took a defining turn when he began working with communities in Limpopo to secure land claims. Through decades of legal struggles and deep human connections, he eventually married into the BaMankge rainmaking lineage and integrated into the rural villages of Sekhukhuneland.
